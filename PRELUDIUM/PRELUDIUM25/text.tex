\section{Scientific goal of the project}
Water stable isotopologues are natural tracers of the global hydrological cycle.
Variations in isotopic composition arise from isotope fractionation accompanying
phase transitions and transport processes in the atmosphere.
Consequently, the isotopic composition of atmospheric water preserves information
about the thermodynamic and microphysical history of air parcels, clouds,
and precipitation.

In atmospheric sciences, the water isotopologues most commonly studied are \ce{HDO},
\ce{H2{}^18O}, and \ce{H2{}^17O}.
Their relative abundances differ from that of the dominant isotopologue \ce{H2{}^16O},
leading to measurable fractionation during evaporation, condensation, and mixing processes.
Recent advances in cavity-enhanced laser spectroscopy have enabled continuous,
high-precision measurements of multiple water isotopologues in atmospheric water vapour,
including triple oxygen isotopes, especially \ce{^17O}.

Measurements of the isotopic composition of water vapour below cloud base will provide input
to a particle-resolved cloud model.
The simulated cloud state will subsequently be evaluated against observational data.
The overall objective is to reconstruct cloud isotopic conditions using numerical simulations.

A central question is whether isotopic measurements provide sufficient information
to constrain cloud microphysical processes and achieve isotopic closure within
a prognostic particle-based framework.
More broadly, it remains unknown whether isotopic closure can be achieved with current
measurement capabilities and advances in particle-based cloud modelling.
If so, an equally important question is what constitutes the minimal mathematical model,
numerical implementation, and observational framework required to achieve it.

The project addresses the following research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textbf{Q1}] Can isotopic closure be achieved in a particle-based
        cloud model using currently available isotopic observations?
    \item[\textbf{Q2}] Which cloud microphysical processes leave
        identifiable signatures in the isotopic composition
        of atmospheric water?
    \item[\textbf{Q3}] To what extent do isotopic measurements constrain
        key cloud properties such as supersaturation,
        phase partitioning, and mixing?
    \item[\textbf{Q4}] What is the minimal physical and numerical
        framework required to achieve isotopic closure
        while maintaining predictive skill?
\end{enumerate}

Cloud microphysical processes remain one of the largest sources of
uncertainty in weather prediction and climate projections \parencite{IPCC_2023}. 
Processes such as condensation, evaporation, entrainment,
and phase transitions between liquid water and ice strongly
influence cloud lifetime, precipitation formation,
and radiative properties.
Yet many of these processes cannot
be directly observed and therefore remain poorly constrained
in atmospheric models \parencite{2013_Grabowski_Wang}.

Stable water isotopologues provide a unique observational tracer 
of cloud evolution.
During phase transitions, isotopic fractionation modifies
the relative abundance of isotopologues in vapour, liquid water, and ice \parencite{2017_Lamb_EtAl}.
Consequently, isotopic composition contains information about 
the thermodynamic and microphysical history of air masses and
clouds \parencite{2013_Bolot_EtAla}.

Theoretical descriptions of equilibrium and kinetic isotope
fractionation are well established,
and stable water isotopologues are routinely represented
in global and regional atmospheric models \parencite{1984_Jouzel_Merlivat, 2016_Galewsky_EtAl}.
These isotope-enabled models have significantly improved
understanding of large-scale moisture transport and
the atmospheric hydrological cycle \parencite{2020_Risi_EtAl}.
However, most existing approaches rely on Eulerian formulations
and bulk cloud parameterisations,
limiting their ability to resolve the particle-scale processes
responsible for generating isotopic signatures.

At the same time, particle-based cloud models have emerged 
as a state-of-the-art framework for studying cloud microphysics \parencite{2009_Shima_EtAl, 2019_Grabowski_EtAl}.
By explicitly representing aerosol particles, cloud droplets,
and ice particles, these models provide detailed descriptions of condensation,
evaporation, freezing, and other microphysical processes.

Despite these advances, isotopic composition has rarely been 
considered within particle-based cloud modelling,
and its potential as a constraint on cloud evolution remains
largely unexplored.

A fundamental unresolved question is whether isotopic observations
contain sufficient information to constrain cloud microphysical
processes and achieve isotopic closure.
Analogously to cloud condensation nuclei closure studies,
isotopic closure can be understood as the ability to consistently explain observed
isotopic composition using a physically based description of cloud evolution \parencite{2021_Knopf_EtAla}.
While isotopic measurements are increasingly available and
process-resolving cloud models are becoming increasingly sophisticated,
no systematic framework currently exists for assessing isotopic
closure in cloud systems.

The proposed project addresses this gap by investigating isotopic
closure using process-resolving cloud simulations and isotopic observations
from in-cloud measurements and laboratory experiments.
The project is pioneering because it introduces isotopic closure
as a research objective in cloud physics and establishes
a link between isotope-enabled observations and modern cloud microphysics.
The results will provide insight into the information content
of isotopic measurements, improve understanding of isotopic signatures
generated by cloud processes, and contribute to the development
of new observational constraints for atmospheric modelling.

\textbf{PR1}
introduction of the Bolin Number was proposed for modelling isotopic composition in particle-based models.
The concept of this dimensionless quantity originates from \textcite{1958_Bolin} in the context
of the analysis of tritium (\ce{^3H}) concentration in precipitation.
The Bolin number represents relation between diffusion processes for one isotopologue to the other,
in these studies it is expressed as specific mass change of heavy isotopologue (\ce{HTO}, \ce{HDO})to the light

This formulation allows the isotopic evolution problem to be reduced to a compact dimensionless representation
that captures equilibrium and kinetic fractionation regimes within a unified framework.

\textbf{PR2}
Evolution of isotopic composition in the vapour and activated droplet 
is modelled using Super-Droplet Method \cite{2009_Shima_EtAl}.
First implementation of the model was developed within PySDM and validated against theoretical benchmarks,
including the isotopic equilibrium curves presented in \cite{1994_Gedzelman_Arnold}.
Initial results confirmed that the proposed formulation reproduces
expected equilibrium conditions under limiting assumptions.
The results are presented in \ref{fig:bolin}.

ensuring correctness of physical constraints and numerical implementation.
A preliminary comparison with theoretical predictions demonstrated consistency

\subsection{Scientific gap}
Despite recent progress, three key limitations remain.
First, isotopic fractionation in ice processes (deposition, sublimation, freezing)
is not yet represented in a unified particle-based framework.
Second, feedbacks between cloud microphysics and isotopic composition 
are not fully resolved in a fully prognostic mixed-phase system.
Third, there is no systematic validation against coordinated field and laboratory datasets
spanning all relevant phases.

These limitations prevent a rigorous assessment of isotopic closure in realistic cloud systems
and motivate the development proposed in this project.

\section{Project planning, milestones and risk management}

\subsection{Measurable objectives (SMART goals)}

The project is structured around a set of specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) objectives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{S1: Isotope-enabled SDM framework development.}
    Extend the PySDM model to include prognostic multi-isotopologue evolution in vapour, liquid, and ice phases.
    \textit{Success criterion:} fully functional implementation with unit tests verifying mass conservation and equilibrium fractionation limits.
    \textit{Timeframe:} Months 1--6.

    \item \textbf{S2: Ice-phase isotopic microphysics implementation.}
    Implement isotopic fractionation during deposition, sublimation, freezing, and melting processes.
    \textit{Success criterion:} reproduction of theoretical equilibrium fractionation curves for ice-vapour and liquid-ice transitions within 5\% relative error.
    \textit{Timeframe:} Months 4--12.

    \item \textbf{S3: Controlled numerical experiments.}
    Perform box, parcel, and column simulations to isolate isotopic response to supersaturation, temperature, and droplet size distribution.
    \textit{Success criterion:} quantified sensitivity maps of isotopic composition with respect to key microphysical parameters.
    \textit{Timeframe:} Months 8--16.

    \item \textbf{S4: Isotopic closure assessment.}
    Evaluate whether observed vapour isotopic composition constrains cloud microphysical evolution in simulated environments.
    \textit{Success criterion:} defined closure metric (error norm or information gain) showing reduction of uncertainty in retrieved cloud state variables.
    \textit{Timeframe:} Months 12--20.

    \item \textbf{S5: Validation against observational and laboratory data.}
    Compare model results with CAESAR aircraft campaign data and laboratory experiments in collaboration with NSF NCAR.
    \textit{Success criterion:} statistically consistent reproduction of observed isotopic variability within measurement uncertainty bounds.
    \textit{Timeframe:} Months 16--24.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Risk analysis and mitigation strategies}

The project involves several scientific and computational risks:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R1: Model complexity and numerical instability.}  
    The extension of particle-based microphysics to multi-isotopologue and mixed-phase processes may introduce numerical stiffness and instability.
    \textit{Mitigation:} modular implementation within PySDM, extensive unit testing, and progressive model activation (vapour $\rightarrow$ liquid $\rightarrow$ ice).

    \item \textbf{R2: Insufficient observational constraints.}  
    Available isotopic datasets may not fully constrain all microphysical processes.
    \textit{Mitigation:} combine aircraft (CAESAR) and laboratory datasets; focus on process-oriented validation rather than full state reconstruction.

    \item \textbf{R3: Parameter identifiability (non-uniqueness of closure).}  
    Multiple microphysical configurations may reproduce similar isotopic signatures.
    \textit{Mitigation:} explicit uncertainty quantification, sensitivity analysis, and definition of closure in terms of information gain rather than unique inversion.

    \item \textbf{R4: Computational cost of particle simulations.}  
    High-resolution SDM simulations with isotopic tracking may become computationally expensive.
    \textit{Mitigation:} use reduced box/parcel models for sensitivity studies and selective high-resolution simulations for key cases.

    \item \textbf{R5: Theoretical uncertainty in ice-phase fractionation.}  
    Ice-phase isotopic processes are less well constrained in literature.
    \textit{Mitigation:} implement multiple competing parameterisations and evaluate their impact in ensemble simulations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Isotopic closure}
The central methodological objective is the concept of isotopic closure, defined
as the ability to reconstruct cloud microphysical history from observed isotopic composition.
This will be approached using both forward and inverse strategies: 
(i) forward simulations of cloud evolution from prescribed environmental conditions, 
and (ii) assessment of whether observed isotopic signals can constrain cloud processes. 
Comparison between simulated and observed isotopic fields will be used to evaluate 
the validity and limits of isotopic closure in mixed-phase cloud systems.

\subsection{Theoretical constraints}

\subsection*{Particle-based (Lagrangian) cloud microphysics}
suitable for extending
Cloud systems are represented using a Lagrangian particle-based framework in which cloud droplets,
ice particles, and aerosol particles are represented by computational particles (super-droplets).
Each particle carries prognostic information about mass, velocity, thermodynamic state, and isotopic composition.
This allows explicit representation of microphysical processes such as condensation, evaporation, deposition,
freezing, and collisions without relying on bulk parameterisations.

\subsection*{PySDM: Super-Droplet Method (Monte Carlo algorithm)}

The numerical core of the model is the Super-Droplet Method (SDM), a Monte Carlo particle-based
algorithm for cloud microphysics.
SDM enables efficient simulation of stochastic droplet-aerosol interactions while maintaining
computational scalability.
In this project, the method is extended to include prognostic tracking of multiple water isotopologues,
coupled consistently with vapour, liquid, and ice phase transitions.
This extension preserves the original SDM structure while enriching particle attributes with isotopic composition.

\subsection*{Open-source scientific software and implementation}
The model is implemented within the open-source PySDM framework using Python,
with selected components potentially extended in Julia for performance-critical modules.
The software architecture follows modular design principles, enabling flexible testing
of physical parameterisations and isotopic fractionation schemes.
The framework is designed for reuse and extension by the scientific community, particularly
for isotope-enabled cloud modelling studies.

\subsection*{Reproducible computational research}
The project follows reproducible research principles, including version control (GitHub),
continuous integration (GitHub Actions), and automated testing of physical consistency.
Unit tests include verification of conservation laws, dimensional consistency, and limiting-case behaviour.
This ensures numerical robustness and traceability of scientific results.
All simulation setups and analysis workflows will be provided as executable notebooks
to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

\subsection*{Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis}
Model behaviour will be systematically analysed using sensitivity studies and uncertainty quantification methods.
Key parameters include temperature, relative humidity, supersaturation, droplet size distribution,
ice fraction, and initial isotopic composition. 
Ensemble simulations will be used to assess robustness of isotopic signatures and model predictions. 
Where appropriate, uncertainty propagation techniques will be applied to quantify
the influence of parameter uncertainty on isotopic evolution.
